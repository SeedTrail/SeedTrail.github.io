Learning from known events

Before I can search for unknown events, I must first learn what an event looks like. I begin with moments that are already documented in human history: political crises, elections, gaming phenomena. I observe how they surfaced on YouTube and not through meaning or intention, but through traces. Words repeated. Uploads accelerating. Categories drifting from their usual rhythm. For each event, I compare specific signals, keywords tied to the moment, with broader category activity. I am not measuring importance or impact. I am learning patterns: sudden bursts of language, abnormal production rates, deviations from expected behavior. These known events form my reference memory. They are my training ground, where I learn to recognize the digital signature of something happening in the world.

When events leak beyond their category

Events rarely remain contained. A political election appears in gaming videos as satire. A game release triggers debate far beyond entertainment. What begins in one place spreads elsewhere. I track how signals migrate across categories, how words cross boundaries, how creators respond indirectly to the same external shock. Through keyword diffusion and behavioral shifts, I observe communities reacting, sometimes knowingly, sometimes not. Events are no longer isolated points in time. They become networked phenomena, spreading unevenly across the platform, leaving traces where they are least expected.

Defining criterias to classify events

After observing many cases, I begin to generalize. Events differ. Some are sudden and unique. Others are predictable and recurrent. Yet beneath these differences, common structures emerge. I formalize these structures into detection criteria: temporal sharpness, keyword concentration, cross-category spread, creator alignment. These features do not describe what an event means, nor why it matters. They describe how an event reveals itself through collective digital behavior.

Feb 10 2018

Discovering unseen events

I am now confronted with unlabeled time. There are no dates, no names, no historical references. Only signals: fluctuations, correlations, anomalies. Using the criteria I have learned, I evaluate whether an external event likely occurred and what kind it might have been. I do not predict reactions. I perform inference. I estimate the probability that observed patterns correspond to a real-world event. In doing so, the platform becomes an archive. Not of videos, but of history itself.
