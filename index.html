From events to networks

When events leak beyond their category

I worked all night and am starting to get to know the data well. Tonight, I had a revelation that kept me awake, as it will completely transform my approach. Until now, I have focused on understanding and observing events alone, which revealed little about human reactions, engagement or overall significance, for the simple reason that events rarely remain confined. Discussions about a political election can appear in gaming videos in many different forms, ranging from satire to fiery debate. A game release triggers debate far beyond entertainment. What begins in one place might without any doubt spread elsewhere. I now have to track how events occurrence migrate across categories, how words cross boundaries, how creators respond whenever directly or indirectly to the same external shock. I want to determine in which categories the event is being discussed: is it an important event that is spreading widely throughout the YouTube ecosystem, or is it limited to certain specific categories of content creators?
My idea is as follows: I will analyze events based on their date of occurrence, then search for videos containing keywords associated with the event (in their description or title) within two months of its occurrence. For each video containing the keywords, I will extract the video's category. I think this will allow me to understand how the event is appropriated by different categories and how it shapes the YouTube ecosystem.
Ready for processing. Capsule saved.

Events Spill Over into Unrelated Categories

Sankey signal analysis

I have managed to decode the data stream and analyse topics occurrence by categories. The Sankey diagram maps the presence of events across YouTube’s categorical architecture. Each flow traces how an external event fractures and emerges into the various categories in video content, revealing the platform’s adaptive response to event disruption. Political events and news related events rarely remain confined to the category News and Politics. Electoral cycles, referendums, and crises leak into three main categories that are Gaming, Entertainment and People & Blogs, where creators translate institutional narratives into commentary, satire, and lived experience. The 2016 U.S. Election and Brexit function as high-energy nodes, generating echoes far beyond traditional news formats. Compared to the protests in Hong Kong, elections or political changes in the West seem to have a greater impact and resonance on the platform than social movements (MeToo, Black Lives Matter). Honestly, I am extremely surprised to see that video games feature in most of the videos and screenshots related to Brexit. Were the early 21st-century humans so attached to their consoles? I imagine that video games could have been an opportunity for the free expression of opinions. I must say that I am beginning to seriously doubt the seriousness of my ancestors in the face of such serious events.
On their side, gaming events operate as cultural multipliers. Phenomena such as Pokémon GO or the FIFA 17 launch propagate into Music, Sports, and Travel & Events, demonstrating how play transcends its origin and reorganizes creative expression across communities. When it comes to gaming, the distribution between categories seems more even, with no less than 5 of them sharing similar shares in content creation, whereas for News and Politics, the gap between the three categories with the most videos and the others is striking. I notice something else: certain events occupy a much more important place than others. Brexit and Star Wars Battlefront attract a great deal of attention.
Signal rerouted. Meaning reassembled…

Signals and Disruptions: Absolute and Relative Event Impact in the YouTube Interface

Detecting unseen events

From the invisible to the visible

Time is no longer unlabeled. Dates remain. Words remain. Categories remain. What is missing is attribution. I know when something happened, which terms surged, and where on the platform activity concentrated. What I do not know is what these signals refer to in the external world. The event itself has no name here, only a footprint. From earlier sections, I have learned how events manifest digitally. They compress attention in time, concentrate language, mobilize many creators simultaneously, and often escape their original category. These recurring patterns form detection criteria. They do not explain meaning or causality. They describe how collective behavior reorganizes in response to something outside the platform. In this section, I apply these criteria to moments whose real-world referent is unknown. The timeline becomes interactive: each anomaly marks a potential event. When selected, it reveals its internal structure, keywords that rose together, categories that reacted, creators that aligned, without revealing why. Only after detection do I allow interpretation. I select several of these signals and attempt retrospective inference: can the platform’s traces alone suggest what kind of event occurred? Can dates and words, without labels, be sufficient to recover fragments of history? This is not prediction in the forward sense. It is inference without prior knowledge. Here, YouTube is no longer a mirror of documented events. It becomes a system that records them, even when their names are forgotten.
