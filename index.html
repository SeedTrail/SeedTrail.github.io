A dive into methodology

Okay, here is what I am going to do. I want to observe how events appear on YouTube, not through their meaning or intention, but through their traces: repeated words, accelerated downloads, categories that deviate from their usual rhythm. For each event, I will compare specific signals (keywords related to the moment) with the broader activity of the categories in which they appear. I am truly focusing on learning how to recognize patterns: sudden explosions of language, abnormal production rates, deviations from expected behavior. These known events constitute my reference memory. They are my training ground, where I learn to recognize the digital signature of something happening in the world. To do so, I rely on several complementary analyses. First, I will use a difference-in-differences approach in which I will compare the change in video volume for content related to an event with that of a control group not associated with the event. This allows me to isolate what changes specifically because an event has occurred, rather than because the platform itself has become more active. Next, I will normalize these variations using Z-scores, measuring the extent to which post-event activity deviates from what was normal before the event occurred. This will, fingers crossed, allow me to compare different events, even when their baseline visibility is very different. Finally, I will perform placebo tests by assigning random dates around the event and measuring the apparent impact of each one. This will generate a distribution of “false shocks.” When the actual event stands out with a high Z-score, it suggests that the observed disruption is not accidental, but rather the trace of a genuine break in collective attention. Together, these methods will hopefully teach me what a disruption looks like. Let’s dive in !

A dive into methodology

I woke up this morning with a second revelation: I have to search for alignment. Every single day on the platform contains millions of words, but most of them are noise: common sentences, recycled templates, or ritual language of “subscribe,” “watch,” “link in description.” An event is not characterised by the presence of words; which thought completely reshaped my vision. An event is the sudden coordination of words across many independent creators. I need to look for and to extract such coordinations. I asked for advice from my colleagues who encouraged me to progress as follows: I will focus on working with channels categories for event prediction. I then will divide the data into daily snapshots, and gather all videos being produced on this day by category, merging titles and descriptions into a single text stream. Hum... Well.. I just realized that I will have to face a methodological constraint! I cannot let high-output channels dominate the signal. I don’t want to measure volume, but consensus of themes within those categories. The only solution I see is to give each channel a common weight of 1, regardless of how many videos it uploads to compensate for content volume differentiation. From these categories of text-streams, I will extract bigrams, or pairs of words. Single words are too ambiguous, too easy to trigger accidentally . Two-word phrases are more stable: “democratic national,” “black ops,” “merry christmas.” They behave like small narrative atoms. The question becomes simple: On a given day, how many distinct channels use the same bigram? To answer this I will create and compute a daily score: for each bigram, I am counting how many channels contain the bigram in their text-stream (binary presence, not frequency). I will keep the top bigrams of the day snapshot and measure the resulting consensus around the topic (consensus being defined by dividing the number of channels using the top bigram by the total active channels that day). Consensus appears to me as an event’s fingerprint. It truly represents the collective alignment of content creators around a topic mimicking external impact on the outside world. Finally, I will have to evaluate potential candidate events by thresholding. Some days top bigrams rise far above their own baseline while others are more stable. I will then define the threshold using the category distribution (mean + multiple standard deviations). When the consensus exceeds this threshold, I will flag the day as a potential event. I can’t wait for the first results !!
